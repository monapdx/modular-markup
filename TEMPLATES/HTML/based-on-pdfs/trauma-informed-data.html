Problem statement

Platforms, institutions, and policies can intensify harm when they ignore gendered realities.

One-size-fits-all systems routinely fail the people they claim to protect. When policy, moderation, reporting practices, or institutional procedures are built around a neutral user who does not actually exist, the result is predictable: the most marginalized people bear the greatest burden.

Effective policy design requires more than broad statements of principle. It requires definitions that reflect lived experience, enforcement that can be trusted, and structures that recognize the specific ways harm is distributed.

Recommendations

Move from vague values to concrete operational rules.

  • Explicitly define gender-based coercion, harassment, and abuse in policy language rather than hiding them inside generic categories.
  • Center the experiences of survivors and marginalized groups when designing reporting, escalation, and support systems.
  • Distinguish between aggregated notification, support-oriented intervention, and punitive referral so that care systems do not become surveillance systems.
  • Require informed consent, transparency, and clear explanations whenever sensitive data collection or high-risk reporting pathways are involved.
  • Publish regular transparency reporting that shows not just overall enforcement, but disparate impacts across vulnerable populations.
Why this matters

Design choices are never neutral when the subject is power.

Reports like this do more than summarize data. They shape what institutions are willing to see, how the public understands harm, and whether people affected by that harm are treated as credible. A trauma-informed report should lower the barrier to understanding without lowering the seriousness of the issue.

Best use cases

Survey reports, public-interest research, advocacy explainers, health equity dashboards, feminist policy briefs, domestic violence resources, and nonprofit annual research publications.
